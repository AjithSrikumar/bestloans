## RBI Prepayment Rules in 2026 — The Foundation

As per the Reserve Bank of India's guidelines (in force since 2012 and still applicable in 2026): **no prepayment or foreclosure charge can be levied on floating-rate home loans for individual borrowers**.

This means if your home loan has a floating interest rate (which most do in 2026), you can prepay any amount at any time at **zero cost**.

---

## Fixed-Rate Loans: Different Rules

For fixed-rate home loans, lenders may charge a prepayment penalty — typically **2%–3% of the amount prepaid**. Always read your loan agreement carefully.

If your loan has a partly fixed / partly floating structure, the penalty may apply only to the fixed portion.

## Part Prepayment vs Full Foreclosure: Which to Choose?

**Part prepayment:** making a lump sum payment above your regular EMI. Two choices after:
- **(a) Keep EMI same and reduce tenure** — saves more total interest; highly recommended
- **(b) Reduce EMI and keep tenure same** — lowers monthly burden but costs more interest

**Full foreclosure:** paying off the entire outstanding principal in one go. Best done when you have a significant windfall (inheritance, property sale proceeds, large bonus). Triggers the NOC collection process.

## Prepayment ROI Calculation — Is It Better Than Investing?

Effective return from prepayment = your home loan interest rate (post-tax).

In April 2026:
- If your rate is 7.5% and you're in the 30% tax bracket with Section 24(b) claim, effective post-tax cost is approximately **5.25%**
- A fixed deposit earning 7%–7.5% (pre-tax) or a liquid mutual fund earning 7%+ might marginally beat this
- However, if your loan rate is **8.5%+ (older loan), prepaying almost always beats any risk-free investment**

## Top 4 Prepayment Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Depleting your emergency fund** — always maintain 3–6 months of expenses in liquid savings before making any lump sum prepayment

2. **Paying off low-rate loan while holding high-rate debt** — if you have a personal loan at 14%, pay that off first

3. **Ignoring tax deduction loss** — prepaying a loan in early years reduces Section 24(b) interest in future years; calculate net benefit

4. **Not confirming zero charges in writing** — get written confirmation from your lender before large prepayments, even if they claim zero charges
